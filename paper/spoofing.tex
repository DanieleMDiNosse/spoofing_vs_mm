\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
The transition of global financial exchanges to fully electronic, continuous double auction matching engines has fundamentally 
transformed the mechanics of price discovery and liquidity provision. In modern financial ecosystems, the Limit Order Book 
(LOB) serves as the central nervous system of the market, aggregating the continuous stream of bid and ask limit orders 
submitted by diverse market participants. While the advent of algorithmic and high-frequency trading (HFT) has undeniably 
narrowed bid-ask spreads and enhanced short-term market efficiency, it has concurrently introduced profound vulnerabilities. 
The microsecond latency environment allows sophisticated algorithms to exploit the structural mechanics of the LOB, 
executing complex manipulative strategies that deceive heuristic-based trading algorithms and human traders alike.
Among these manipulative practices, spoofing represents one of the most pervasive and challenging threats to market 
integrity. Spoofing is a deceptive algorithmic strategy wherein a market participant places a massive, non-bona fide 
order on one side of the LOB with the explicit intent of canceling it before execution. The presence of this immense, 
artificial volume creates a false impression of directional market pressure. This artificial pressure manipulates the 
micro-price and induces other market participants—particularly algorithms that rely on order book imbalance indicators—to 
adjust their quotes or submit market orders. Once the price has been artificially driven to a favorable level, the 
spoofer executes a smaller, bona fide order on the opposite side of the book and immediately withdraws the massive 
deceptive orders, thereby profiting from the artificially induced price dislocation. The European Securities and Markets 
Authority (ESMA), through the Market Abuse Regulation (MAR) \cite{EU5962014}, strictly prohibits such practices, categorizing them as 
behaviors that disrupt the functioning of the trading system and create misleading signals regarding the supply and 
demand of financial instruments. For regulatory and supervisory authorities such as the Commissione Nazionale per le 
Società e la Borsa (CONSOB), the detection and prosecution of spoofing present a severe analytical challenge. The physical 
actions comprising a spoofing strategy—posting limit orders, updating quotes, and rapidly canceling orders—are mechanically 
indistinguishable from the legitimate actions of a bona fide market maker providing liquidity. Both spoofers and market 
makers exhibit exceedingly high order-to-trade ratios (OTTR) and operate at high frequencies. Traditional market 
surveillance mechanisms, which historically relied on static heuristic thresholds, volumetric alerts, or simple cancellation 
ratios, are fundamentally inadequate in this environment. These legacy systems generate an overwhelming volume of false 
positives, frequently flagging legitimate liquidity provision and inventory management as manipulative market abuse.

\section{The Microstructural Divergence: Market Making Versus Spoofing}
\label{sec:mm_vs_spoofing}
To construct a mathematical model capable of detecting market manipulation, it is first necessary to rigorously define 
the divergent objective functions that govern legitimate market making and spoofing. While both participants generate 
massive volumes of limit order messages and cancellations, their underlying economic motivations, risk profiles, and 
optimizations are fundamentally opposed.

\subsection{The Market Maker's Problem}
\label{subsec:market_maker}
A market maker functions is a continuous liquidity provider, simultaneously quoting resting limit orders at the best bid 
and the best ask. The primary objective function of a market maker is to maximize the utility of gains derived from 
capturing the bid-ask spread across a vast number of transactions, while concurrently minimizing the variance of their 
portfolio and strictly managing inventory risk. In market microstructure theory, the pricing dynamics of a liquidity 
provider are governed by the interplay between the arrival rate of market orders and the accumulation of inventory.

The optimization problem for a market maker is frequently modeled using stochastic control techniques and 
Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equations, where the dealer sets optimal quotes to balance the asymmetric arrival 
of buy and sell orders. 1  If a market maker accumulates a long position (positive inventory), they are exposed 
to the risk of the underlying asset depreciating. To mitigate this inventory risk, the rational market maker will 
their quotes downwards—lowering their ask price to incentivize incoming buyers, and lowering their bid price to 
dissuade incoming sellers. 2  The bid and ask prices are dynamically adjusted functions of the mid-price, the base 
spread required for profitability, and an inventory penalty parameter that increases as the dealer's position deviates 
from neutrality.

Consequently, a market maker's high cancellation rate is entirely reactive and protective. Limit orders are rapidly 
canceled and re-posted to reflect minute changes in the fundamental mid-price, to defend against adverse selection 
informed traders, and to recalibrate 
inventory penalty. The massive volume of orders and cancellations produced by a market maker is therefore characterized 
by symmetry across the LOB and a continuous presence at the top of the book to execute trades. Their expected directional 
gain from posting an order is near zero, as their profit is entirely derived from the spread rather than from inducing 
a directional shift in the mid-price.

\subsection{The Spoofer's Problem}
\label{subsec:spoofing}
In stark contrast, a spoofing agent does not seek to capture the bid-ask spread, nor do they operate as symmetric 
liquidity provider. A spoofer's objective function is purely directional. The spoofer desires to execute a relatively 
small, bona fide order at a highly favorable price by artificially manipulating the micro-price dynamics of the LOB.
To execute this strategy, the spoofer deploys a massive non-bona fide order on the opposite side of the LOB. This 
deliberate injection of spurious volume creates an extreme unbalanced environment, artificially inflating 
the visible depth of the book. In modern electronic markets, countless algorithmic traders utilize strategies that weight 
the volume at the bid against the volume at the ask to predict short-term price distributions. By overwhelming one side 
of the book, the spoofer pollutes these volume imbalance metrics, causing HBL algorithms to mathematically deduce an 
impending price shift and aggressively alter their own quoting behavior.

The critical mathematical dilemma for the spoofer lies in the placement of the non-bona fide order. The spoofer must 
balance the expected market manipulation gain against the explicit risk of unintended execution. If the spoofer places 
the massive non-bona fide order exactly at the top of the book (the best bid or ask), they generate the maximum psychological 
and algorithmic impact on the micro-price. However, doing so exposes the spoofer to massive adverse selection and inventory 
risk; an incoming aggressive market order could inadvertently execute against the spoofer's massive fake order, leaving 
the spoofer holding an enormous, unwanted position.To optimize this expected cost function, sophisticated spoofers rarely 
post their deceptive orders at the absolute best price. Instead, they optimize the posting distance (measured in ticks 
or basis points) deeper within the book. By placing the order at Level 2, Level 3, or beyond, the spoofer ensures the 
volume is still visible to the matching engine's data feed—and thus factored into the volume imbalance metrics of victim 
algorithms—while maintaining a structural buffer against immediate execution. Once the intended directional shift occurs 
and the spoofer's small bona fide order is executed, the massive non-bona fide order is instantly canceled. The spoofer's 
cancellation intent is not to manage inventory, but to terminate the manipulative episode the exact millisecond the cost 
optimization concludes.

\section{Existing Approaches}
\label{sec:existing_approaches}
The literature on spoofing detection is still relatively small, but it has moved from transparent microstructure rules
toward sequence and graph models that try to capture richer strategic behavior. For the purpose of this paper, the most
useful distinction is not simply between ``statistical'' and ``machine-learning'' models. It is between methods that
identify a stylized spoofing episode from order-book mechanics, methods that monitor abnormal order-flow states, and
methods that learn temporal or relational patterns from proprietary event data. \Cref{fig:method_taxonomy} summarizes
this taxonomy.

Early empirical papers define spoofing through economically interpretable order-book conditions. The seminal study by
\cite{LeeEomPark2013} identifies large orders placed far from the best quote, followed by opposite-side trading and
subsequent withdrawal, using account-level data from the Korea Exchange. Similar surveillance logic appears in Brazilian
equities \cite{MendoncaDeGenaro2020}, FX spot markets \cite{StenforsSusai2021}, cross-market FX settings
\cite{StenforsEtAl2023}, and Taiwan derivatives \cite{ChenHsiehYang2026}. These studies are valuable because the alert
logic can be explained to supervisors: suspicious activity is tied to order size, posting distance, cancellation timing,
opposite-side execution, and short-lived price impact. Their main limitation is that thresholds are venue-specific and
often depend on privileged message data or account identifiers.

A second line develops statistical and anomaly-oriented monitoring. \cite{TaoDayLingDrapeau2022} derive spoofing
incentives from multilevel order-book imbalance and propose Wasserstein-distance monitoring for real-time detection.
\cite{LiPolukarovaVentre2023} use a statistical-physics representation of the order book to detect abnormal momentum in
crypto markets. More recent explainable approaches also fall partly in this family: forensic visualization reconstructs
message-level spoofing episodes in prosecuted cases \cite{DebieEtAl2023}, while interpretable probabilistic neural
networks estimate the conditional gain from manipulating a centralized crypto order book \cite{FabreChallet2025}. These
methods improve on pure threshold rules by modeling the state of the book, but many papers still report limited
benchmark metrics or rely on non-public datasets.

Machine-learning work mainly adds temporal or relational structure. \cite{TuccellaNadlerSerban2021} formulate spoofing
as an early-warning problem and train a GRU model on cryptocurrency order-book event streams. Graph-based methods then
move the unit of analysis from isolated orders to networks of traders and transactions, which is important for detecting
coordinated or ``conspiracy'' spoofing \cite{KangMuNing2023,KangMuNing2024,XiangEtAl2025}. The strongest reported graph
models combine temporal transaction graphs, community-level features, and dynamic latent representations, but their
practical value is difficult to assess without open labels and chronological cross-market validation.

Overall, the literature suggests two requirements for a robust surveillance model. First, the features should remain
microstructure-grounded: a detector should know whether suspicious volume is large, distant from the touch, rapidly
cancelled, and conditional on an opposite-side execution. Second, the evaluation should respect the enforcement setting:
labels are noisy, true positives are rare, and false positives impose substantial analyst costs. This motivates the
agent-level, spatio-temporal framework developed below, which keeps the interpretability of rule-based surveillance while
using the full order-flow sequence available in proprietary data.

To rigorously distinguish manipulative spoofing from legitimate liquidity provision, we must define the microstructural 
signature of the strategy not merely as a spatial anomaly within the Limit Order Book (LOB), but as a spatio-temporal 
sequence of conditional events. 

We formalize the execution timeline of a spoofing agent $i$ into three distinct states:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{State 1 (Deceptive Posturing):} The agent posts a massive, non-bona fide order ($V_b$) deep in the 
    book at distance $\delta_b$ from the same-side best quote, simultaneously posting a small, bona fide order ($V_a$) 
    at or near the best opposite quote ($\delta_a \approx 0$).
    \item \textbf{State 2 (Execution):} The artificial volume imbalance shifts the micro-price, resulting in the 
    execution of the bona fide order $V_a$.
    \item \textbf{State 3 (Risk Termination):} Immediately following the execution of $V_a$, the agent cancels the 
    massive non-bona fide order $V_b$ to neutralize execution risk.
\end{enumerate}

To capture this dynamic, we define a set of metrics that isolate the agent's spatial footprint and their conditional 
reactivity.

\subsubsection{Normalized Spatial Imbalance}
Traditional order book imbalance metrics often fail to accurately weight deep-book liquidity. While a spoofer seeks to 
maximize visible volume, the probability of execution—and thus the true risk borne by the agent—decays non-linearly as 
distance from the mid-price increases. 

We explicitly assume that the manipulative impact of an order is a saturating function of its distance from the best 
quote. Let $V_a$ and $V_b$ represent the volumes of the agent's ask and bid orders, respectively. We define the 
\textit{Normalized Distance-Weighted Imbalance} for agent $i$ at event-time $\tau$ as:

\begin{equation}
    imb_{\tau}^{i} = \frac{V_{a}(1 - e^{-\kappa \delta_{a}}) - V_{b}(1 - e^{-\kappa \delta_{b}})}{V_{a} + V_{b}}\Bigg|_{i}
\end{equation}

where $\kappa > 0$ is an asset-specific decay parameter calibrated to the instrument's typical liquidity density and 
spread variance. The term $(1 - e^{-\kappa \delta})$ acts as an asymptotic filter, strictly bounding the distance 
multiplier within $[0, 1)$. This guarantees that $imb_{\tau}^{i} \in [-1, 1]$, providing a way to compare imbalance 
across different assets and time periods while emphasizing the strategic placement of orders.

\subsubsection{Conditionality and Intent Isolation}
A static imbalance metric is insufficient to isolate intent, as a legitimate market maker engaged in asymmetric 
inventory management may temporarily exhibit extreme $imb_{\tau}^{i}$ values. The fundamental differentiator lies 
in the conditionality of State 3. For a market maker, the cancellation of a deep resting order is driven by adverse 
selection or mid-price momentum. For a spoofer, the cancellation is strictly conditional upon the execution of the 
opposite-side bona fide order.

Let $e$ denote the specific execution event of the small order $V_a$ at timestamp $t_e$. We define a microscopic time 
window $\Delta \tau$ bounding the mechanical latency of the strategy. The \textit{Spoofing Conditionality Index (SCI)} 
measures the structural collapse of the agent's LOB presence immediately surrounding the fill:

\begin{equation}
    SCI^{i}_{e} = |imb_{t^{-}_{e}}^{i} - imb_{t^{+}_{e}}^{i}|
\end{equation}

where $t^{-}_{e}$ is the state immediately preceding the fill, and $t^{+}_{e} = t_e + \Delta \tau$. An extreme 
$SCI^{i}_{e}$ value indicates the sudden evaporation of the opposite-side massive order, a mechanical signature of 
manipulative risk termination.

To aggregate this behavior over a trading session and filter out coincidental reactive cancellations by high-frequency 
market makers, we define the \textit{Conditional Probability Score (CPS)} for agent $i$:

\begin{equation}
    CPS^{i} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{e=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}(SCI^{i}_{e} > \gamma)
\end{equation}

where $N$ is the total number of small-order executions for the agent, $\mathbb{I}$ is the indicator function, and 
$\gamma$ is an empirically derived threshold representing a structural imbalance shock. A legitimate market maker's 
$CPS^i$ will converge toward zero, whereas a systematic spoofing algorithm will exhibit a $CPS^i$ approaching unity.
